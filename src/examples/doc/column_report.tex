\section{Overview}
% ============================================================================

The study compares two backend idealizations of the \emph{same} physical column
(Section~\ref{sec:specimen}):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Reference model} --- a force-based fiber \texttt{forceBeamColumn} cantilever
      (Section~\ref{sec:fiber}).
\item \textbf{Lattice model} --- a 2D assembly of axial truss struts generated on a structured
      \texttt{gmsh} node grid, with longitudinal and transverse reinforcement represented as
      steel struts on shared nodes (Section~\ref{sec:lattice}).
\end{itemize}

Four analysis cases are reported, each run for both models:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Case & Loading & Constitutive setting \\
\midrule
Pushover (linear)     & monotonic lateral displacement & Elastic \\
Pushover (nonlinear)  & monotonic lateral displacement & Concrete02 + Steel02 \\
Dynamic (linear)      & base acceleration (sine, El Centro) & Elastic \\
Dynamic (nonlinear)   & base acceleration (sine, El Centro) & Concrete02 + Steel02 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The lattice strut area is calibrated so that the lattice and the fiber column share the same
initial lateral stiffness $K_0$ (Section~\ref{sec:calib}); this same calibration fixes the
fundamental period used by the dynamic cases. All quantities use a consistent kip, inch, second
unit system.

\label{sec:lattice}

\paragraph{Length-regularized concrete (crack-band).}
Both softening branches of the strut concrete law are regularized by the strut length $L$, so
that the dissipated energy is mesh-objective:
\[
E_{ts}=\frac{f_t^2 L}{2G_f}\ \ (\le 0.5E),
\qquad
\varepsilon_{U}=\varepsilon_{c0}+\frac{2G_{fc}}{(f_c+f_{cu})\,L}\ \ (\ge \varepsilon_U^{\text{grade}}),
\]
with a residual compression plateau $f_{cu}\ge 0.2 f_c$. The fracture energies are
$G_f = 4.0\times10^{-3}\unit{kip/in}$ (tension) and $G_{fc}=1.5\unit{kip/in}$ (compression).
This yields four concrete materials (two zones $\times$ two strut lengths):

\paragraph{Mass.}
Nodal mass is lumped by tributary volume ($\rho\,t\,A_\mathrm{trib}/4$ per quad corner) from the
same grid used to draw the struts. The total lattice self-mass is
$\sum m = 1.1664\times10^{-2}\unit{kip\,s^2/in}$ ($=\rho V$, $V=24\times144\times15$). For the
dynamic cases a top lumped mass $P/g = 0.4658\unit{kip\,s^2/in}$ (the axial load as tributary
seismic mass, $g=386.4\unit{in/s^2}$) is added at the top nodes.

\section{Stiffness calibration}
\label{sec:calib}
% ============================================================================

The single calibration parameter is the concrete strut cross-sectional area $A$ (uniform over all
concrete struts), chosen so the lattice's initial lateral stiffness $K_0$ equals the fiber
column's. The reinforcement areas are fixed by the specimen and are not calibrated.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Nonlinear cases:} a plain elastic lattice (reinforcement omitted) has
      $K_0 \propto A$, so a single solve gives $A$ from the fiber-column $K_0$.
\item \textbf{Linear cases:} the full RC elastic lattice (concrete $+$ rebar) has a stiffness that
      is monotone but not proportional in $A$ (rebar adds a parallel stiffness), so $A$ is found by
      a secant root-find on $K_0(A)-K_0^{\mathrm{ref}}$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Analysis procedures and parameters}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Pushover}

A constant-gravity stage (axial $P$) is applied first and held; the lateral displacement of the
control node is then increased monotonically under displacement control while the base shear
(sum of horizontal base reactions) is recorded.

\subsection{Dynamic (seismic time history)}

After the held-gravity stage, the base is driven by a \texttt{UniformExcitation} (ground
acceleration record) along $x$. Two records are used: a harmonic acceleration tuned to resonance
with the structure's fundamental period $T_1$, and the recorded El Centro 1940 NS ground motion.
The record intensity is tuned on the (inexpensive) fiber column to a target peak roof drift; the
\emph{same} scaled record then drives the lattice.

In the \emph{linear} cases all materials are \texttt{Elastic}, so both models stay linear at any
amplitude. In the \emph{nonlinear} cases the intensity is set so the peak roof drift (about
$1.2\unit{in}$) exceeds the effective yield drift of both models --- the elastic limit is
$\approx 0.5\unit{in}$ for the fiber column and $\approx 0.6\unit{in}$ for the lattice (peak shear
$\div\,K_0$; see the pushover curves in Section~\ref{sec:res-pushover}) --- so both the lattice and
the fiber beam--column respond in the nonlinear range, leaving a permanent residual drift
(Table~\ref{tab:residual}).
